Now that we have demonstrated the theoretical link between FEM and PINN, you might begin to question why despite being mathematically equivalent, the two methods yield different results. This blog addresses that question. By bringing the theoretical commonalities into a visual sense you will begin to understand why we say that SLP is adaptively meshed FEM in disguise.

# How do they recreate solutions?

In the previous blog we discussed how elements in FEM have compact support, whereas SLP is not constrained in its optimisation and therefore each nodal activation spans the whole domain. Intuitively you can already start to imagine how each method reconstructs the solution, have a think of what that would look like and then have a look at the image below.

So, we see that for FEM, each element is non-zero only in the region in which it acts, by summing all these individual contributions over the whole domain the PDE can be reconstructed! Now put your attention towards the ELM recreation and its nodal activations, we see that each node activates across the whole domain (these activations actually have the profile of a sigmoidal activation, but the rescaling of the second y-axis squished them so now they look linear), just like in FEM, we can sum the contribution of each node, then the PDE is recreated! This style of reconstruction applies even to higher dimensional PDEs, but more emphasis needs to be placed on the expressivity of the first layer weights, which is responsible for deciding the activation direction. More on this later.

From this simple case, you can already see how well the SLP recreates the solution, surpassing the FEM's performance with the same number of optimised parameters (only 4 out of 10 nodal activations are shown). So why do we not replace all FEM solvers with PINN solvers and call it a day? Hoorah AI has taken over! FEM is no more... Well no. PINNs come with their own set of problems. We introduced two of these in the last post, but for this post we will focus on PINN's Achilles' heel, spectral bias.

# The curse of global support

By construction, FEM obeys the local information structure of the PDE due to the compact nature of its trial function. In contrast, the global support of SLP activations allow for information travel through the whole domain. On paper that sounds great because the optimiser can adaptively choose where to refine activations, but it is ultimately this flexibility which curses PINNs. This global support faciltates inconsistencies with how information is represented in the domain described by the PDE.

More practically, the optimisation of the SLP is described by a linear-least squares task defined by the governing PDE. The network parameters are then varied to minimise this loss. Nature always chooses the path of least resistance, the optimiser chooses to do the same! First, the dominant structure in the solution are optimised for since the loss is minimised most effectively by representing the large structures (if you look back to the previous image you will notice that the first 4 nodes recreate the peaks of the solution), then the rest of the nodes are reserved for representing the finer details of the solution. This inherent bias to solve larger structures first is ultimately the downfall of PINNs, because during training the optimiser might get stuck optimising incorrectly. It would be like refining your mesh in the freestream to better capture the boundary flow - just because you can and it might actually improve overall performance, it does not mean it is the correct strategy. Does this mean PINNs are fundamentally flawed? Not necessarily, we can leverage their adaptability.

## Hope is not lost

By varying the first layer weights the activations can be stretched or contracted, and the first layer biases can be used to activate the sigmoids at specific locations in the domain. We can manually choose where to refine - which we calculatedly coined adaptive meshing in disguise!! 

Let us see this in action. In the first task, we have a high frequency sinusoidal forcing Poisson problem and in the second task we have a multi-scale forced Poisson problem. In both tasks, we first initialised the first layer with constant $1$ weights and uniformly distributed the first layer bias across the whole domain. Essentially creating a uniform mesh in FEM terms. The node-output weights were then optimised in a linear least squares fashion. So what was the result? Well, not so good. In both cases, the SLP was not able to reconstruct the high frequency components of the solution this was especially evident in the multi-scale case, where the SLP captured the low frequency very well but poorly captured the high frequency.

So as we would do in FEM, let us change our mesh. We can scale the activations using a scalar multiplier, we can also shift the mesh using the first layer bias. If we can do it in such a fashion that activations are small enough to capture fine featuers and sigmoids are activated at correct locations, then this should allow us to recreate the whole solution profile. Guess what, it worked! Don't believe us? look for yourself.

The sharper-eyed among you may have noticed that FEM remained accurate in both tests - thanks to its local information respecting compact support. Due to the simplicity of this task, even a typical ADAM-LBFGS training approach would likely allow the SLP to recreate the solution accurately. However, the purpose of these examples is to highlight the spectral bias inherent in PINNs. In higher dimensions, where thousands more parameters and deeper networks are required, and tasks might have hundreds of scales to solve, these tasks reveal how spectral bias can make optimisation landscapes far more challenging to navigate. By illustrating these challenges, we begin to demystify the often opaque inner workings of PINNs.

## So can we really call PINNs meshless?

Long story short and contrary to the [meshless claims](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.engappai.2025.112044) of the PINN ultras, we boldly claim that they are meshed, but this mesh is baked in the opacity of the PINNs architecture. In fact, [Sikora et al. (2024, J. Comp. Sci.)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jocs.2024.102340) in their paper showed that a non-uniform distribution of points, outperformed the classic uniform distribution style PINN.

But knowing this comes at a cost, an initially thought advantage to PINNs was the perceived simplicity of their implementation. This knowledge that SLPs are adaptive mesh FEM in disguise disrupts this simplicity, we now see that without previously knowing, we revisit a similar task to difficulties of generating meshes in numeric methods. Where converging to a correct solution, actually requires some form of knowledge of the solution itself!


# A quick look at higher dimensions

We briefly mentioned how expressivity of the first layer weights is important for high dimensional tasks. To understand this a bit better, consider a 2D implementation for the SLP we described. Again for simplicity, we consider a simple Poisson BVP. The input is now 2D, subsequently, the matrix of first layer weights is no longer a column vector but rather a matrix of $\mathbb{R}^{H \times 2}$, where each column in this matrix activates an orthogonal direction.

We now invite you to think about how the solution will be recreated in 2D, while keeping in mind that SLP is adaptive meshed FEM in disguise. 

The solution is reconstructed by planes activated at different points in the domain, and how are these planes defined in space? With the first layer weights and biases! Each weight pair is responsible for a single plane and the corresponding bias is responsible for activating that plane at a certain distance from the origin. To demonstrate this, let us initialise the first layer weights as columns of $1$ and the bias as a uniform distribution defined by the longest length scale in the domain as we did in the 1D case we saw previously. Before looking at the solution try guess what this would look like.

Essentially, a collection of planes parallel to each other activated uniformly along the entire length of the domain! Do not believe us? See for yourself!

Again we see that the first layer parameters are synonymous to meshing in FEM! In this case, we created a uniformly distributed mesh of parallel cells. We meshed a 2D problem, with a 1D mesh! So let us use our knowledge of how SLPs recreate solutions. For simplicity we can randomly initialise the first layer weights, but we will keep the bias as a uniform distribution defined by the longest length scale in the domain. This random initialisation makes sure that the variation of weights between the two orthogonal directions are not similar. There is now any variation between the planes, that the whole problem space is covered.

# Summary 

In this post, we numerically demonstrated the implications of what we called adaptively meshed FEM in disguised. Particularly, we focused how the globally supported nature of SLP activations is a double-edged sword, providing great flexibilty but coming at the cost of a lack of convergence guarantee and well-conditioning. 

We showed that the first layer weights and biases are responsible for controlling the width, direction and location of the activations. Through the simple Poisson BVP tasks, SLP's flexibility was used to demonstrate the simplest form of spectral bias in PINNs. When the SLP was initialised in such a fashion that it was directly synonymous to a uniform mesh in FEM, the PINN struggled to converge to a correct solution. Ultimately converging to a lower frequency solution, we can say that the PINN demonstrated spectral bias. However, controlling the activation width allowed through adjust first layer weights allowed for increased SLP expressivity. In the tasks, the sigmoid activations were contracted such that they were able to represent the small scale features in the problem. This would be similair to adapting a mesh in FEM to account for areas of high variation.

The ability to control the width, direction and location of activations puts into question whether PINNs are truly meshless. We choose to crash the meshless party and say that SLPs are not meshless, they are just globally supported FEM with a neural network's flexibility, where the mesh is hiddedn in the opacity of the model's architecture. This means if you are using PINNs, you are still meshing, you are just doing it implicitly! And that means the same old challenges of mesh generation, adaptivity and prior knowledge still apply.

So, if PINNs are just adaptively meshed FEM in disguise, the question is not whether to use them, but rather how to use them better. How can we leverage their adaptivity without falling into the same old traps? Perhaps the answer lies in information theory. But enough for today, that is for a future blog post.
