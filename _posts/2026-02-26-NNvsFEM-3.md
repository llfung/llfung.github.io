Now that we have demonstrated the theoretical link between FEM and PINN, you might begin to question why despite being mathematically equivalent, the two methods yield different results. This blog addresses that question. By bringing the theoretical commonalities into a visual sense you will begin to understand why we say that SLP is adaptively meshed FEM in disguise.

# How do they recreate solutions?

In the previous blog we discussed how elements in FEM have compact support, whereas SLP is not constrained in its optimisation and therefore each nodal activation spans the whole domain. Intuitively you can already start to imagine how each method reconstructs the solution, have a think of what that would look like and then have a look at the image below.

So, we see that for FEM, each element is non-zero only in the region in which it acts, by summing all these individual contributions over the whole domain in a way that fulfills the PDE, the solution can be reconstructed! Now put your attention towards the ELM recreation and its nodal activations, we see that each node activates across the whole domain (these activations actually have the profile of a sigmoidal activation, but the rescaling of the second y-axis squished them so now they look linear), just like in FEM, we can sum the contribution of each node, then the solution to the PDE is recreated! This style of reconstruction applies even to higher dimensional PDEs, but more emphasis needs to be placed on the expressivity of the first layer weights, which is responsible for deciding the activation direction. More on this later.

From this simple case, you can already see how well the SLP recreates the solution, surpassing the FEM's performance with the same number of optimised parameters (only 4 out of 10 nodal activations are shown). So why do we not replace all FEM solvers with PINN solvers and call it a day? Hoorah AI has taken over! FEM is no more... Well no. While the activation function in NN being globally supported might have helped the SLP to use less nodes compared to FEM, it also comes with some costs. One of the cost is that the training cost scales worst in more complex solution. The other is the violation of information structure, both of which we have briefly discussed in the [previous post](../NNvsFEM-2). Here, we will focus on another Achilles' heel of PINN -  spectral bias.

# The curse of being too expressive
It's often argued that FEM is better than PINN because it doesn't have the spectral bias that plagued PINN. That is actually not entirely true. FEM is also inherently bias in its spectrum - the mesh density determines the highest frequency FEM can resolve. The idea that FEM is not as spectrally-biased is because that bias is explicitly and trivially shown and actively removed by users.
By construction, FEM's trial function obeys a certain mesh. This implies that if we increase the mesh density, the equivalent first-layer weight in the SLP representation of FEM also increases. This is why if we have higher frequency signal in the solution, all we have to do in FEM is to increase mesh density such that we resolve the solution in the frequency it needs to be. In other words, the act of meshing to convergence by the user is the way FEM remove the spectral bias. 

SLP, in constrast, do not have this correlation with a mesh built-in. In some sense, it is described as "meshless" (more on that later). This flexibility is the main reason for its higher expressivity, and why we can get away with an appearantly smaller SLP to resolve the same solution above comapred to FEM. However, it is also this expressivity that is the source of spectral bias in NNs. The fact that the first-layer weight is no longer tied to a mesh implies that the optimisation of the weight towards the right spectrum to resolve a solution is now the job of the optimiser. Yet, it's not guaranteed that the optimiser can always optimise towards the right spectrum. Hence, spectrum bias can be thought as a failure for the optimiser to converge to the global minima of a loss.

In practice, we can think of this failure more mechanistically. Say we are to optimise a solution that is composed of a high frequency signal and a low frequency signal.  Nature always chooses the path of least resistance, the optimiser chooses to do the same! First, the dominant structure (the low frequency signal) in the solution are optimised for since the loss is minimised most effectively by representing the large structures , then the rest of the nodes are reserved for representing the finer details of the solution. This inherent bias to solve larger structures first is ultimately the downfall of PINNs, because during training the optimiser might get stuck optimising incorrectly. It would be like refining your mesh in the freestream to better capture the boundary flow - just because you can and it might actually improve overall performance, it does not mean it is the correct strategy. Does this mean PINNs are fundamentally flawed? Not necessarily, we can leverage their adaptability.

## Hope is not lost

By varying the first layer weights the activations can be stretched or contracted, and the first layer biases can be used to activate the sigmoids at specific locations in the domain. We can manually choose where to refine by varying the first layer bias, and the width of the region that is changing most rapidly, which correlates with the spectrum. This is effectively the same action as changing the mesh in FEM - which is why we call [training an SLP adaptive meshing in disguise](../NNvsFEM-2)!! 

Let us see this in action. In the first task, we have a high frequency sinusoidal forcing Poisson problem and in the second task we have a multi-scale forced Poisson problem. In both tasks, we first initialised the first layer with constant $1$ weights and uniformly distributed the first layer bias across the whole domain. Essentially creating a uniform mesh in FEM terms. The node-output weights were then optimised in a linear least squares fashion. So what was the result? Well, not so good. In both cases, the SLP was not able to reconstruct the high frequency components of the solution this was especially evident in the multi-scale case, where the SLP captured the low frequency very well but poorly captured the high frequency.

So as we would do in FEM, let us change our mesh. We can scale the activations using a scalar multiplier, we can also shift the mesh using the first layer bias. If we can do it in such a fashion that activations are small enough to capture fine featuers and sigmoids are activated at correct locations, then this should allow us to recreate the whole solution profile. Guess what, it worked! Don't believe us? look for yourself.

The sharper-eyed among you may have noticed that FEM remained accurate in both tests - thanks to its local information respecting compact support. Due to the simplicity of this task, even a typical ADAM-LBFGS training approach would likely allow the SLP to recreate the solution accurately. However, the purpose of these examples is to highlight the spectral bias inherent in PINNs. In higher dimensions, where thousands more parameters and deeper networks are required, and tasks might have hundreds of scales to solve, these tasks reveal how spectral bias can make optimisation landscapes far more challenging to navigate. By illustrating these challenges, we begin to demystify the often opaque inner workings of PINNs.

## So is PINNs meshless?
Yes and No. It's "meshless" in the sense that the weights is not longer tied to a mesh, which earns PINN higher expressivity. This expressivity, along with the overcoming of the curse of dimensionality through the depth of MLP (we will discuss that further in another post), is the main advantage PINN has over FEM. Yet, it's not entirely "meshless" in that any arbitrary architecture can represent any arbitrarily complex solution at arbitrary precision. There is still a "mesh", that is the NN architecture itself, that needs to be tuned according to the solution. This tuning will likely scale with the complexity of the solution it represents. Hence, in many ways, **the NN architecture is the new "mesh"**, and all the difficulties of generating meshes in numeric methods will also likely applies to the design of NN architecture. In particular, like FEM, to converge to a correct solution, we actually requires some prior knowledge of the solution itself!

# A quick look at higher dimensions

We briefly mentioned how expressivity of the first layer weights is important for high dimensional tasks. To understand this a bit better, consider a 2D implementation for the SLP we described. Again for simplicity, we consider a simple Poisson BVP. The input is now 2D, subsequently, the matrix of first layer weights is no longer a column vector but rather a matrix of $\mathbb{R}^{H \times 2}$, where each column in this matrix activates an orthogonal direction.

We now invite you to think about how the solution will be recreated in 2D, while keeping in mind that SLP is adaptive meshed FEM in disguise. 

The solution is reconstructed by planes activated at different points in the domain, and how are these planes defined in space? With the first layer weights and biases! Each weight pair is responsible for a single plane and the corresponding bias is responsible for activating that plane at a certain distance from the origin. To demonstrate this, let us initialise the first layer weights as columns of $1$ and the bias as a uniform distribution defined by the longest length scale in the domain as we did in the 1D case we saw previously. Before looking at the solution try guess what this would look like.

Essentially, a collection of planes parallel to each other activated uniformly along the entire length of the domain! Do not believe us? See for yourself!

Again we see that the first layer parameters are synonymous to meshing in FEM! In this case, we created a uniformly distributed mesh of parallel cells. We meshed a 2D problem, with a 1D mesh! So let us use our knowledge of how SLPs recreate solutions. For simplicity we can randomly initialise the first layer weights, but we will keep the bias as a uniform distribution defined by the longest length scale in the domain. This random initialisation makes sure that the variation of weights between the two orthogonal directions are not similar. There is now any variation between the planes, that the whole problem space is covered.

# Summary 

In this post, we numerically demonstrated the implications of what we called adaptively meshed FEM in disguised. Particularly, we focused how the globally supported nature of SLP activations is a double-edged sword, providing great flexibilty but coming at the cost of a lack of convergence guarantee and well-conditioning. 

We showed that the first layer weights and biases are responsible for controlling the width, direction and location of the activations. Through the simple Poisson BVP tasks, SLP's flexibility was used to demonstrate the simplest form of spectral bias in PINNs. When the SLP was initialised in such a fashion that it was directly synonymous to a uniform mesh in FEM, the PINN struggled to converge to a correct solution. Ultimately converging to a lower frequency solution, we can say that the PINN demonstrated spectral bias. However, controlling the activation width allowed through adjust first layer weights allowed for increased SLP expressivity. In the tasks, the sigmoid activations were contracted such that they were able to represent the small scale features in the problem. This would be similair to adapting a mesh in FEM to account for areas of high variation.

The ability to control the width, direction and location of activations puts into question whether PINNs are truly meshless. We choose to crash the meshless party and say that SLPs are not meshless, they are just globally supported FEM with a neural network's flexibility, where the mesh is hiddedn in the opacity of the model's architecture. This means if you are using PINNs, you are still "meshing" implicitly when you design an architecture that works. And that means the same old challenges of mesh generation, adaptivity and prior knowledge still apply.

So, if PINNs are just adaptively meshed FEM in disguise, the question is not whether to use them, but rather how to use them better. How can we leverage their adaptivity without falling into the same old traps? Perhaps the answer lies in information theory. But enough for today, that is for a future blog post.
